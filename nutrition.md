# Nutrition Plan

**Constraint that shapes everything:** one kitchen, six people, same meals, food is expensive. So this isn't a separate "diet." It's the same family dinner, portioned and sequenced so it works for your goal.

## The numbers (starting point)

Based on 6'2", 38, 210 lb, active. These are estimates we verify against your week-one scale trend and adjust.

- **Maintenance:** ~3,000–3,100 kcal/day given your walking, lifting, and riding.
- **Target intake:** **~2,300 kcal/day** average. A ~700–800 deficit, aggressive enough to move the scale, gentle enough to protect muscle and your energy for the kids.
- **Protein:** **~190 g/day.** The single most important number for keeping muscle while the fat comes off. Roughly 0.9 g per pound of bodyweight.
- **Water:** ~100 oz baseline, more on ride days.

Carbs and fat fill the rest. Put most of your carbs around training and riding, when your body uses them best.

## Straight talk on the 10-pound target

It's on the table. Here's the honest shape of it so the scale doesn't mess with your head:

- **Week 1 drops fast** (often 4–6 lb). Most of that early number is water and stored carb, not fat. Real, but not all fat.
- **After that, ~1.5–2 lb/week of actual fat** at this deficit.
- Net over ~3.5 weeks: seeing **sub-200 by Aug 1 is very achievable.** Getting all 10 to be pure fat is the aggressive end, which is exactly why protein and the lifting matter. Push the deficit much harder and you start spending muscle, which is the opposite of what you want.

Weigh yourself the same way every morning (after the bathroom, before eating) and watch the **weekly average**, not the daily wiggle.

## The family-meal method

You don't cook separate meals. You build your plate in this order from whatever's on the table:

1. **Protein first, a real portion.** A palm-and-a-half to two palms.
2. **Vegetables second, pile them on.** They're your volume and fullness for almost no calories.
3. **Starch third, a moderate scoop.** Rice, potato, pasta, bread. Not banned, just not the base of the plate.
4. **Fats light.** Go easy on the oil, cheese, and sauces that the kids can have freely.

Same lasagna night, same taco night. Your plate just tilts toward the protein and veg.

## Cheap protein that stretches across a family

Ranked loosely by cost per gram of protein:

- **Eggs** — breakfast anchor, cheap, filling.
- **Chicken thighs / whole chickens** — cheaper than breast, more flavor, roast a couple at once.
- **Ground beef (80/20) and ground turkey** — tacos, chili, pasta sauce, meatballs.
- **Canned tuna and sardines** — pantry protein, near zero prep.
- **Dried beans and lentils** — pennies per serving, stretch the meat, feed everyone.
- **Greek yogurt and cottage cheese** — high protein, kid-friendly, great snacks.
- **Whole milk** — cheap calories and protein for the kids, a measured glass for you.
- **Whey protein** — cheapest way to close a protein gap when the day runs out. One scoop ~25 g.
- **Oats** — not protein, but the cheap, filling carb base that keeps this affordable.

## A sample day (~2,300 kcal, ~190 g protein)

- **Breakfast:** 3 eggs + 1 cup oats + banana. (~500 kcal, ~30 g)
- **Lunch:** big bowl, chicken thigh + rice + frozen veg + hot sauce. (~650 kcal, ~48 g)
- **Snack:** Greek yogurt + whey scoop, or cottage cheese. (~350 kcal, ~50 g)
- **Family dinner:** your plate, protein-forward, veg-heavy, moderate starch. (~750 kcal, ~55 g)
- **Water all day, coffee black.**

On a long-ride day, add ~200–300 kcal back (a bigger dinner portion or a second yogurt) so your legs and recovery don't suffer. Shift it around your schedule. The two rules that carry it: protein at every meal, veg for fullness.

## Five cheap family dinners that fit

1. **Sheet-pan chicken thighs + roasted veg + potatoes.** One tray, feeds everyone.
2. **Taco night** with seasoned ground beef, beans to stretch it, loaded with lettuce, tomato, salsa. Your plate: more meat and beans, fewer chips.
3. **Big pot of chili** (beans + ground turkey/beef). Cheap, freezes, high protein.
4. **Pasta with meat sauce**, extra lean beef, jarred sauce bulked with frozen veg. Moderate your pasta scoop.
5. **Rice-and-egg bowls / fried rice** with whatever protein is around and a bag of frozen veg.

## Habits that quietly do the work

- Protein and veg before the starch, every plate.
- No calories in cups. Water, black coffee, zero-cal drinks.
- Don't out-snack the deficit at night. If you're hungry, reach for protein (yogurt, jerky, a boiled egg).
- If a day goes off the rails, the next meal is the reset. No spiral.

## Room for drinks and treats

Build these in on purpose. Once you hit your protein (~190 g) and load the veg, you've got roughly **150–250 kcal/day of flex**. Spend it daily on a small treat, or bank it across the week for a night out. Either works, as long as the weekly average deficit holds.

**Treats.** Fit them in the numbers. A couple squares of dark chocolate, a scoop of ice cream, a cookie with the kids. Portion it, keep the protein and veg intact, and move on. The protein-and-veg-first habit does most of the policing for you, since you rarely have much room left to overdo it.

**Alcohol.** Allowed, with eyes open about the exchange rate. It runs ~7 kcal/g, and while your body clears it, fat burning pauses. It also tends to dent your sleep and recovery, which Oura will show you the next morning, and it loosens the reins on late-night snacking, which is usually where the real damage hides.

So spend it deliberately:
- A workable target during this push: **up to 3–4 drinks a week**, ideally on one social night rather than a nightly pour.
- Cheaper-by-the-calorie picks: spirit with a zero-cal mixer (~100 kcal), light beer (~100 kcal), dry wine (~120 kcal/glass). IPAs and sugary cocktails run 200–350+ each, so those eat your whole flex budget in one glass.
- Put drinks on a long-ride day when there's more room, protect your protein that day, and keep the water going.

**The one tradeoff to hold in your head:** more drinks this month means slower loss. If the weekly scale average stalls, the drinks are the first lever we pull back, before we ever cut food further. Once you're under 200 and shift to maintenance, this all opens up.
